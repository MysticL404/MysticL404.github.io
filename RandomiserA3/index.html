I initially had the idea of making the user interface simple enough so the user wouldn't need to waste time and jump straight into the randomiser so my specific design decision was to make the interaction and randomiser HTML's a single page with no scrolling needed. With the :root set with multiple variables, I experimented many unique colour palette choices that both HTML's could work with, originally starting with orange and ending with a strong light blue and dark blue design. The design I first had in mind when presenting the ideas was to create a playful feedback summoning simulator, in short, creating a randomiser that picks out an image from a variety of options randomly. I felt strong with the idea but I soon came to a roadblock which was figuring out how to make multiple random images appear at the same time and making them disappear at the same time when inputting the randomiser button once more. I brainstormed solutions and while the original idea had lots of feedback to offer, the playfulness didn't shine the way I wanted it to. Clicking a single button and not being in control was a boring and uncomfortable experience so I decided on making a simulator capable of making a dream team.

Since I'm still new to javascript, W3schools would teach me the basics of coding a progress bar, a primary way of providing feedback to the user so they know their input did something and now know to wait still the progress bar hits 100%. Progress bars while functioning as feedback could also play the role of being playful as instead of just sitting there, the user actually has something to watch though this could be bias for the audience.

Melvin Adekanye helped me learn about the image_array, the main focus of the randomiser as it allows the buttons to randomly choose an image from the wide selection given. This is where the playfulness of the website comes in as some may want to create their dream team seriously or mess around with the webpage. Since there are 5 image_shower slots set, there are many potential outcomes and basically every user who may find themselves using it will have a different and unique outcome from their peers. This proved to be a much more playful experience overall.

A unique design choice I wanted was inspired by mobile gacha games as well as the following link which helped me achieve designing it. Educative.io was able to basically show me how I could create a slideshow between images. Many gacha games have something like this for their featured units or characters so I wanted to create something similar for the interaction page to give the users an idea on what there is to offer. There isn't much feedback here but the animations are enjoyable to watch.

Lastly is arguably the most crucial add-on for feedback which is the animation played upon clicking the buttons. The obvious things like :hover and :active are implemented although an animation is required for when the images appear, a simple fade in animation is basically all that's required so the users are aware that the input worked in the situation they randomise the same image multiple times. Delftstack was where I discovered how to javascript this into the webpage and like before, it would require me to make 5 seperate functions for each button.

Originally whilst working on this assignment, I had a bunch of characters from offical games or movies but that has been changed to simple animal stock photos sourced from Pixabay for free to avoid any copyright issues.
